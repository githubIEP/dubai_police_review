Introduction

This report presents visualizations and summary statistics for all indicators in the GPSI (Global Policing and Security Indicators) dataset.

Key Findings for United Arab Emirates

Overall Performance:

  • UAE ranks 14 out of 156 countries globally on the GPSI (2023)
  • GPSI Score: 0.844
  • This places UAE in the top 8.4% of all countries - an excellent performance

Strongest Areas:

  • Security and Readiness Score: Ranked 10 (Top 5.8%)
  • Top 3 Individual Indicators:
    • Resilience: Top 3.9% globally
    • Safe Walking Alone: Top 5.8% globally
    • Cybersecurity: Top 5.8% globally

Communications Consideration: Ranks vs Percentiles

This is not a methodological issue - the GPSI methodology is sound and the scores are robust. However, how we interpret and communicate these results requires careful consideration.

The Challenge: UAE is positioned in a dense cluster of high-performing countries. Specifically:

  • 9 countries are within ±1.2% of UAE’s score
  • A 1.2% score change (well within measurement uncertainty) could shift UAE’s rank by 7 places down or 2 places up
  • These score differences are smaller than typical survey margins of error and can result from minor methodological adjustments

What This Means for Communications:

Option 1: Emphasize Ranks (e.g., “UAE ranks 14th globally”) - ✓ Simple, intuitive message - ✗ Sensitive to small, statistically insignificant changes - ✗ May fluctuate notably between index versions despite stable performance - ✗ Can mislead stakeholders about meaningful differences between countries

Option 2: Emphasize Percentiles (e.g., “UAE is in the top 8.4% globally”) - ✓ More stable across minor methodological changes - ✓ Better reflects true relative performance clusters - ✓ Less sensitive to measurement uncertainty - ✗ Slightly less intuitive for general audiences

IEP Recommendation: Focus on percentile-based communication for the overall GPSI score and domain scores, while acknowledging the rank for context. For example: “UAE performs in the top 10% globally on the GPSI (ranked 14th out of 156 countries), demonstrating excellent policing and security outcomes.”

This approach provides transparency about UAE’s position while avoiding over-interpretation of small rank differences that may not reflect meaningful performance gaps.

Outstanding Data Issues:

  • 4 indicators currently use regional averages rather than UAE-specific data
  • These include: Police Confidence, Mortality in Prison, Police Rate, and Suicide in Prison
  • Providing actual data could improve UAE’s ranking if actual performance is better than the regional average
  • Key opportunity for improvement through data collection initiatives

Review Process

IEP has undergone a thorough review of each indicator and has made the following changes:

  • Dropped Motor Vehicle Injury Rate Indicator - MENA countries were penalized in this survey
  • Edited Government Expenditure on Public Order and Safety - Previously used a linear band that rewarded less spending. However, the global average is around 1.7% of GDP, so it has been benchmarked to this value such that both over-spending and under-spending are penalized
  • Made minor banding changes to crime indicators (Violent Crime and Property Crime)
  • Removed problematic UNODC indicators that are not comparable across countries due to different criminal codes. Property Crime now uses only the Gallup World Poll question

Note on this version: This report focuses on the methodological review of the GPSI. IEP can update some of the indicators with more recent data for the next version of the index.

Understanding Index Stability and Rankings

As discussed in the Communications Consideration above, the GPSI methodology is sound, but rank interpretation requires care due to score clustering among high-performing countries.

This section provides detailed analysis of rank sensitivity - demonstrating why percentiles offer a more stable and meaningful communication approach than absolute ranks. This is not a flaw in the index construction, but rather a natural consequence of many countries achieving similarly high performance levels on policing and security indicators.

Rank Sensitivity: Overall GPSI Score

UAE’s overall rank is highly sensitive to small, potentially insignificant changes in score due to extreme clustering of countries at similar performance levels.

Current UAE Position: Ranked 14 out of 156 countries with a score of 0.844

Extreme Score Clustering:

  • 9 countries are within ±0.01 (±1.2%) of UAE’s score
  • 15 countries are within ±0.02 (±2.4%) of UAE’s score

Asymmetric Rank Volatility:

  • A 1.2% score decrease would drop UAE 7 ranks (from 14 to 21)
  • A 1.2% score increase would gain only 2 ranks (from 14 to 12)

These score differences are well within the margin of error of underlying survey data and methodological choices (e.g., imputation methods, banding parameters, weighting schemes). Small technical changes can cause large, misleading rank swings, while the actual performance remains essentially unchanged.

Key Takeaway: The table above shows how tightly clustered scores are around UAE. The difference between 13th place (South Korea, +0.15%) and 15th place (Austria, -0.43%) is less than 1% - a difference that could easily result from survey sampling error, imputation choices, or minor methodological adjustments. This is why percentiles provide a more stable and meaningful measure than specific rank positions.

Indicator-Level Example: Property Crime

The same communication challenge exists at the indicator level. Consider the Property Crime indicator (percentage reporting money or property stolen from household in the past year).

In 2023, UAE scores 13%, which might seem high. However, 88 countries scored better (lower crime rates), placing UAE at rank 89 out of 156 countries. It’s not that UAE scores poorly on this indicator - rather, the underlying data is highly skewed toward low crime rates globally. Small differences (e.g., 11% vs 13%) are likely within survey margins of error but produce large apparent rank differences.

In this example, UAE ranks 89 out of 156 countries. While 88 countries scored better (lower crime rates), the actual difference between UAE’s 13% and many of these countries is minimal and likely within the margin of error of the survey. This illustrates the same communication principle: rank positions can suggest larger performance gaps than actually exist, while percentiles better capture that UAE performs similarly to the large cluster of low-crime countries.
